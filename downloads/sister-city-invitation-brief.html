Before you use this brief

This is a draft starting point — not a ready-to-send document.

This brief is designed to help initiate a conversation with Frankston–Susono sister-city custodians about a small creative exchange. Before sending any version of this, the following must happen:

  1. The relevant Frankston City Council officer managing the sister-city relationship must review and approve the contact approach.
  2. Any Japanese text, names, or titles must be reviewed by a qualified speaker — do not use machine translation for formal correspondence.
  3. The proposed exchange must be understood as a starting question, not a program pitch. The first step is listening.
  4. If this leads to any formal exchange, a separate cultural and community review process applies — especially for anything involving students or children.

Draft invitation brief

Adapt this before sending. Add names, correct titles, have it reviewed.

Frankston 2035 — Art That Lives Prepared by Blue Snake Studio for Frankston–Susono sister-city review
To[Name/Title of Sister-City Relationship Custodian, Frankston City Council] and [Name/Title of counterpart in Susono, if applicable]
FromBlue Snake Studio, on behalf of the Frankston 2035 — Art That Lives proposal
Date[Date of submission — complete before sending]
SubjectInvitation to discuss a small creative exchange — Frankston 2035

Thank you for your time and for the work you do to maintain the Frankston–Susono relationship.

We are writing to invite a conversation about one small, carefully considered creative exchange that could sit within the existing sister-city relationship — if it is welcome and appropriate.

We are not proposing a program. We are not announcing a partnership. We are not asking for endorsement of anything that has already been designed. We are asking a question: is there a kind of exchange — one artifact, one response, one shared creative prompt — that would be genuinely welcome from the Susono side?

Who we are. Blue Snake Studio is an independent creative studio based in Frankston. We have developed a civic creativity proposal called Frankston 2035 — Art That Lives. The proposal explores ways for Frankston's existing public art, schools, libraries, and community spaces to become more active and generative — for residents, students, families, and teachers.

What we are exploring. One of the four loops in the proposal is a sister-city exchange — the idea that a small, replyable creative artifact could pass between Frankston and Susono participants. This might be a song prompt, a short poster, a classroom response sheet, or a bilingual learning card. The key word is "replyable" — it only counts as exchange if something comes back.

We are at the earliest stage of exploring this. Nothing has been designed. No content has been created. No students are involved yet. We want to understand, first, whether this kind of exchange would be welcome at all.

What we are asking. A short conversation — 15 to 30 minutes — to understand:

  • Whether a small creative exchange is something the sister-city relationship currently has capacity and interest to explore.
  • What kind of exchange would feel appropriate and respectful from the Susono side.
  • What the correct process would be for any formal proposal to proceed.

We are not asking for approval of a program, endorsement of a proposal, language assistance, cultural content, or any commitment at this stage.

A note on scope. If there is interest in exploring further, we would expect any first exchange to be: very small (one classroom, one artifact, one term); reviewed by the appropriate council and education officers; available in both English and Japanese, with qualified translation; explicitly labelled as a proposed exchange, not a confirmed program.

We are genuinely open to being told that this is not the right time, or that the format is not appropriate, or that there are other things the relationship needs first. That is useful feedback, not a failure.

What a minimal exchange could look like

Three options — only if the conversation goes that way.

These are starting ideas, not ready designs. They would need review, translation, cultural input, and council approval before use. They are here to illustrate the scale of what is being proposed — small, replyable, respectful.

Option A — A shared song or chant prompt A simple rhythmic or melodic prompt that students in both cities can respond to in their own language, with their own context. Each response stays in the classroom until reviewed. No public sharing without explicit approval.
Option B — A replyable poster prompt One poster with a single visual prompt — a question or an image — sent to participating students in both cities. Students create their own response poster. Responses are shared between classrooms first, then reviewed before any wider display.
Option C — A bilingual learning card A simple bilingual card with a place-based prompt in English and Japanese — designed with a qualified translator and reviewed by both sides before use. A starting point for language and place exploration, not a cultural content claim.

Important: None of these options involves First Nations content, language, or cultural material. Any First Nations content that might eventually be connected to a sister-city program would require a completely separate, independent, permission-based process guided by Traditional Owner organisations.

Rules for this brief

What must be true before this brief goes anywhere.

  1. This draft has been reviewed by the Frankston City Council officer responsible for the sister-city relationship before sending.
  2. Any Japanese text has been reviewed by a qualified speaker, not machine-translated.
  3. The brief is sent as a question, not a proposal with an expected answer.
  4. If the answer is no, or not now, that is respected immediately and without further contact on the matter.
  5. No students, artworks, content, or cultural material are involved until the conversation has happened and there is clear mutual interest.
  6. Any outcome from this conversation that involves students requires a separate consent and child-safety review process.
