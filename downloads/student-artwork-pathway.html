Student Artwork Pathway

From classroom to public — step by step.

Safe steps for taking student creative work from a classroom activity to a public display. Each step requires a decision and a sign-off before moving forward.

Independent proposal by Blue Snake Studio. Not an official Education Department or school policy document. Check your school's own policies before any public display of student work.

The pathway

Five gates. Each one is a decision point.

Work only progresses to the next stage when the current stage is complete. Do not skip gates.

  1. Gate 1 — Create. Students create work from a local prompt. No personal data (full names, photos, addresses) in the work. No login or student accounts used during creation.
  2. Gate 2 — Teacher review. Teacher reviews each piece for school policy compliance: respect, privacy, accessibility, and suitability. Work that does not pass this review stays in the classroom or is adapted.
  3. Gate 3 — Family consent. Before any work is displayed outside the classroom, the family or guardian receives a plain-English consent note. See the Permission Checklist for the minimum requirements. Work is not displayed publicly without a signed or confirmed response.
  4. Gate 4 — Classroom or library display only. First public display is limited to the school building: a classroom wall, library display, or school corridor. No QR codes, no online posting, no out-of-school display at this stage.
  5. Gate 5 — Expanded display. Any display beyond the school building (local library, council venue, online) requires a separate consent confirmation, an adult-led QR layer if used, and review against the Healthy Technology Charter. No personal data about children enters the public layer.

What counts as public

If anyone outside the class can see it, it is public.

A display is public if it can be seen by anyone who has not been given explicit permission by the student's family. This includes:

  • A corridor, hallway, or foyer visible to parents, visitors, or community members
  • A library display open to the public
  • A council-run venue or event
  • Any website, social media post, or digital display accessible without a login
  • A QR code that links to student work or student names

It does not include:

  • A classroom display seen only by the class and the regular teaching team
  • Work shown to parents at a scheduled parent-teacher event (though consent is still good practice)

What to avoid

Common mistakes in student artwork displays.

  • Full names on public displays. Use first name only or no name if the display is public.
  • Photos of students alongside their work. Never include student photos in any public display without explicit family consent for that photo in that context.
  • Rushing to digital. A corridor display is safer than a website. Start there.
  • Combining class lists with artwork. Do not publish a list of student names alongside their works.
  • Assuming previous consent covers new contexts. Consent for a classroom display does not extend to a website or an external venue.
  • No ending on a QR layer. Any QR code must have a clear ending — it should not loop students into ongoing digital engagement or data collection.

Quick summary

Gate checklist for any public display of student work.

  1. No personal data (full name, photo, address) in the work or caption.
  2. Teacher has reviewed and approved the work for the display context.
  3. Family consent is confirmed in writing for the specific display context.
  4. First display is classroom or school building only.
  5. Any QR layer is adult-led, has a clear ending, and collects no child data.
  6. Any expansion beyond school requires a new consent step.
