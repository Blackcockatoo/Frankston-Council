Lesson Pack Starter

Notice. Make. Share safely.

A 30-minute classroom session linked to Frankston public art or local place. Works for any year level with local adjustment. No login. No student accounts. No personal data collected.

Independent proposal by Blue Snake Studio. Not an official school curriculum or Education Department resource. Always follow your school's own processes.

For teachers

Three steps. 30 minutes. One local prompt.

Each session has three phases. Timing is a guide — adjust for your class.

  1. Notice — 10 minutes. Use one local prompt (below) to focus attention. Students observe, look, or remember a place, object, or person connected to Frankston. The digital layer, if used, supports this step only — a QR code pointing to a photo or map prompt. Then devices go away.
  2. Make — 15 minutes. Students create something in response to the prompt: a sentence, a drawing, a map, a word list, a short poem, a diagram. No screen needed. No typing. Paper and pencil only at this stage.
  3. Share safely — 5 minutes. Students share within the room. Pair-share or small group. Teacher decides what, if anything, goes further — see the Student Artwork Pathway and Permission Checklist before sharing publicly.

Adapting for year level

Pick the prompt format that fits your class.

Foundation–Year 2

One question. One drawing. "Draw what you can see from here." "Draw your way to school."

Year 3–4

Two or three sentence responses. "Describe this place in three lines." "What would you change about this wall?"

Year 5–6

Short paragraph or poem. "Write from the point of view of the mural." "What story does this place need to tell?"

Year 7–10

Analysis or creative response. "What does public art say about who a community values?" "Design a mural for this site."

Choose one prompt

Use one. Adapt for your students and local site.

Pick the prompt that fits your available time, location, and subject. You can use this without visiting a mural — the prompt can work from memory, description, or a class discussion about local place.

  • The bay at this time of year. What is the foreshore doing right now? Describe it or draw it from memory. What sounds, smells, colours, feelings?
  • A wall that asks a question. Find a public mural near the school (or show a photo). What is it saying? What would you add? What question would you put on a wall where strangers could read it?
  • My route to school. Draw or describe the journey from home to school. What do you pass? What do you usually miss? What would a stranger notice that you've stopped seeing?
  • A person who makes Frankston better. Name someone — a person you know, a local business, a volunteer — who makes the local area better. Write three sentences about what they do and why it matters.
  • If this wall could talk. Choose any wall or public surface you know in Frankston. What would it say if it could speak? Write its voice.

Teacher notes

Before, during, and after the session.

Before the session:

  • Choose one prompt from the list above, or write your own local version.
  • Print one student worksheet per student (below), or display the prompt on the board.
  • If you will use any photos or maps, cue them up in advance — screen time is for the notice step only.
  • Decide in advance whether any work will leave the classroom. If yes, complete the Permission Checklist before the session, not after.

During the session:

  • Keep the notice step to 10 minutes. A tight time limit helps students focus.
  • Resist the urge to extend the make step beyond 15 minutes — short constraints often produce better responses.
  • For the share step: pairs, then selected volunteers, then whole class. Keep it light.

After the session:

  • Collect student work or let students keep their worksheets.
  • If displaying publicly: follow the Student Artwork Pathway — every step, in order.
  • Send feedback to Blue Snake Studio if you run this pilot. Subject: "Frankston 2035 — school pilot". Your feedback shapes Stage Two.

Before going public

Check these before any student work leaves the classroom.

  1. Confirm there is no personal data (full names, addresses, photos of students) in or on the work.
  2. Complete the school's standard permission process for displaying student work.
  3. Gather family consent before work appears on any public display or website.
  4. Review the Student Artwork Pathway and Permission Checklist — in that order.
  5. Do not post student work online without explicit school and family approval.
  6. Any QR code linking to student work must pass the Healthy Technology Charter review first.
