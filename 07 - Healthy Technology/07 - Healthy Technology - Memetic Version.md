# 07 - Healthy Technology
## Memetic / High-Impact Version

## Core line

Technology should give attention back.

## Short manifesto

The screen is not the artwork.

The feed is not the community.

The metric is not the meaning.

Frankston is not content.

Frankston is the place. The people. The walk. The school desk. The library table. The mural wall. The conversation after the phone goes away.

Healthy technology opens the door, then gets out of the way.

## Public-facing lines

- Create, then leave the screen.
- Scan less. Notice more.
- The best click is the one that sends you outside.
- No child data. No attention traps. No tricks.
- If it steals attention, it does not belong here.
- If it needs a child profile, redesign it.
- If it feels like gambling, reject it.
- If a parent cannot understand it in one sentence, simplify it.
- Frankston first. Technology second.
- Useful beats shiny.
- A good QR code ends in a real-world action.
- Public art should pull people into place, not into a feed.
- The tool is small. The civic imagination is big.

## Internal decision lines

- Do not optimise for time on screen.
- Optimise for creative action after the screen.
- Do not ask children for data.
- Ask what the prompt helps them notice.
- Do not build a feed.
- Build a doorway.
- Do not chase novelty.
- Build trust.

## One-sentence pitch

Frankston 2035 uses lightweight, no-login, child-safe digital prompts that help people make, notice, walk, learn, and then leave the screen.

## What this protects

- children from extraction
- families from manipulation
- schools from privacy risk
- libraries from tech support burden
- Council from hidden governance risk
- Frankston from being turned into content

## Final line

Do not build monuments. Build makers.

Do not build attention traps. Build attention back.
