## Main Document Draft

### Frankston, 2035

Picture a Year 4 class on a foreshore excursion. A student scans a code beside a mural and the wall opens into a maths lesson — the same geometric pattern painted on the wall, now something they can rotate, solve, and play with on their own screen. Picture a trail through the city told twice — once in English, once in Japanese — because Frankston's sister-city relationship with Susono finally has somewhere to live in public space. Picture a library pack that lets a class build their own piece of public art and see it added to a wall the whole city walks past.

None of this requires Frankston to imagine a future from nothing. It requires Frankston to take what it has already built — foreshore activation, public art, a real sister-city relationship, a growing commitment to First Nations recognition — and give it one new capability: the ability to teach, travel, and be added to.

### Why this matters now

Frankston has spent years investing in public art and creative placemaking. Most of that investment, like public art everywhere, has a natural ceiling: a mural is seen once by most people who pass it, then becomes wallpaper. The civic and educational value of that investment is largely locked inside the paint.

At the same time, Frankston schools are looking for digital learning tools that are safe, local, and genuinely engaging rather than another generic app. The Frankston–Susono sister-city relationship is real but under-resourced with shared content. And there is a growing, genuine civic appetite — among councils across Victoria — to involve First Nations communities in public space on respectful, consent-based terms, rather than performative ones.

These four things — public art, education, sister-city connection, and First Nations respect — are usually treated as four separate council line items. This proposal treats them as one opportunity: give Frankston's public art a second life, and all four threads gain a place to meet.

### What's being proposed

A staged program that adds an interactive, educational layer onto Frankston's existing public art and creative culture — starting with a single pilot, at modest cost, before any larger commitment is considered.

The technology underneath this proposal is not speculative. It already exists, built and tested by Blue Snake Studio, a Frankston-based creative-technology studio (see Section 04 for the full body of work). Council is not being asked to fund the invention of something new. Council is being asked to point an already-working capability at a civic purpose.

### The low-cost pilot pathway

This is not a large funding ask. Stage One of the Roadmap (Section 00) is deliberately small:

- **One** existing piece of public art or foreshore location
- **One** school term of real-world testing
- **One** modest, one-off production budget
- **Zero** ongoing cost commitment beyond the pilot
- **Zero** data collected from children beyond anonymous, aggregate usage counts

Every later stage — schools, libraries, the Susono relationship, First Nations partnership — only proceeds if the pilot earns it, and only on terms set by the communities involved, not by a fixed rollout schedule.

### What Council can do next

Three things, none of which require a budget decision today:

1. **Note this proposal** and indicate whether there is appetite to proceed to a Stage One pilot.
2. **Nominate a single candidate location** — an existing mural, foreshore site, or trail — for the pilot, in consultation with relevant council officers.
3. **Request the Full Proposal and Pilot Project Sheets** (Sections 01 and 08) for a detailed, costed Stage One scope.

That's the entire ask at this stage. Nothing else is being requested of Council today.

### The proof behind the proposal

Everything above rests on one claim worth being able to check: that Blue Snake Studio can actually build this. Section 04 sets out the studio's existing, working body of creative-technology projects — interactive learning tools, tested puzzle engines, and original mathematical systems already built and running, with nothing in this proposal asking Council to fund invention from scratch.
