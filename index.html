What it is

Public art that keeps working after the paint dries.

Frankston 2035 turns public art, foreshore walks, libraries, classrooms, and sister-city relationships into practical creative tools. A mural becomes a lesson. A trail becomes a noticing prompt. A poster becomes a starting point for a teacher, artist, or resident.

This is not a monument plan. The first move should be cheap, testable, and easy to stop if it is not working.

Why Frankston

Frankston is already the raw material.

01

Public art is already here

The proposal builds on existing murals, foreshore activation, and civic pride. It does not treat past work as a problem to fix.

02

Schools and libraries can use it

Every idea must become something a teacher, librarian, parent, or student can actually run with.

03

Susono is a real bridge

Sister-city work should create genuine exchange: shared art, songs, and learning packs — not decorative theming.

04

Country requires care

First Nations content belongs only where Traditional Owner organisations guide whether, when, and how it should be shared.

The Four Loops

The ecosystem is the artwork.

1

Wall to lesson

Public art becomes a worksheet, classroom prompt, or local activity.

2

Walk to attention

QR codes point people to a place to look, notice, sketch — then keep walking.

3

Exchange to artifact

Sister-city material becomes a replyable object: a song, poster, or bilingual class pack.

4

Viewer to maker

Residents and students add work through guided, moderated contribution pathways.

Cultural Protocol

First Nations content begins with permission.

No language, symbols, stories, or claims are invented here. Any cultural content requires guidance from appropriate Traditional Owner organisations, with the right to say no, pause, or remove at any time.

Sister City

Exchange means reply.

Frankston-Susono content should focus on real shared work: class art, song responses, and approved learning packs. The goal is genuine exchange — not decorative theming.

Content principles

The Rules That Protect the Vision

  1. Frankston is the hero.
  2. Blue Snake Studio is the delivery engine, not the headline.
  3. Start small, think generational.
  4. Every big idea needs a low-cost pilot.
  5. Art must be useful, not just beautiful.
  6. Technology must give attention back to real places and real people.
  7. First Nations content is permission-based, respectful, and guided by appropriate Traditional Owner organisations.
  8. Sister City content must focus on real exchange, not symbolic decoration.
  9. Every page points to practical next steps.
  10. The ecosystem is the artwork.
