01 Purpose Statement

Healthy technology helps Frankston become more creative, not more screen-bound.

Frankston 2035 - Art That Lives treats technology as a support layer for real places, real people, and real creativity. The screen is not the artwork. The ecosystem is the artwork.

Healthy technology is technology that helps a person notice, make, learn, connect, or participate - then lets them leave the screen with more attention than they arrived with.

This charter protects children, families, schools, libraries, Council, artists, and public trust from digital tools that look creative but quietly collect data, steal attention, or create duty-of-care risk.

Parent test

This tool helps my child notice or make something in Frankston, then it clearly ends.

02 Main Document Draft

Healthy Technology Charter: Technology Should Give Attention Back

Definition

Healthy technology is short, clear, useful, privacy-first, accessible, and connected to offline creativity. It does not compete with Frankston for attention. It points attention back to Frankston.

Scope

This applies to QR-linked public art pages, classroom tools, library workshop resources, family prompts, poster seed interactions, walking trails, sister-city exchanges, and any B$S digital tool used in this ecosystem.

Create, then leave

The preferred journey is simple: open the prompt, understand the task, make or notice offline, finish. The screen is a doorway, not a destination.

Clear endings

Every experience should make it obvious when the user is done: close this and draw, walk to the next site, print this for class, talk to someone nearby.

Standard Requirement Reason
No infinite scroll Pages must have clear boundaries and endings. Prevents passive consumption.
No dark patterns No manipulative consent, sign-up, navigation, or exit flows. Protects public trust.
No gambling mechanics No loot boxes, streak pressure, random rewards, or chance incentives. Protects children and families.
No login by default Public resources should work without accounts wherever possible. Reduces exclusion and privacy risk.
No child personal data Do not ask children for names, emails, photos, profiles, locations, or identifiers. Reduces legal, ethical, and duty-of-care risk.
Anonymous aggregate metrics only if needed Measure only what helps improve or evaluate the pilot. Avoids surveillance creep.

Design rules

  • One purpose per tool.
  • Explain it to a parent in one sentence.
  • Prefer no-login public access.
  • Design for adults leading groups.
  • Build the exit into every screen.
  • Make the non-digital version strong.
  • Use metrics sparingly.
  • Cut attention-stealing features.

Risk controls

  • Replace child accounts with adult-led modes.
  • Replace personal submissions with paper or teacher-mediated options.
  • Replace live comments with curated workshop outputs.
  • Replace leaderboards with group reflection.
  • Replace always-online tools with printable worksheets.
  • Replace rich tracking with anonymous aggregate counts.

03 1-Page Summary

The screen opens the door. The work happens in Frankston.

Healthy technology should help people engage with public art, classrooms, libraries, homes, and streets without becoming the main event.

Council can support digital tools when they are low-risk, privacy-conscious, accessible, no-login wherever possible, and linked to clear public value.

Schools and libraries need group-safe resources that do not require child accounts, do not collect child data, and can be used without technical support.

Parents need to know what the tool does, what it does not collect, and when the child is meant to leave the screen.

04 Bullet Point Version

Fast rules for any pilot, QR layer, classroom prompt, or public tool.

Hard no

  • No infinite scroll.
  • No dark patterns.
  • No gambling mechanics.
  • No loot boxes or streak pressure.
  • No compulsory accounts for public participation.
  • No personal data collection from children.
  • No hidden tracking.
  • No public leaderboards for children.

Required behaviours

  • No-login by default.
  • Short sessions.
  • Clear exits.
  • Offline creative prompts.
  • Family mode.
  • Classroom mode.
  • Print mode.
  • Plain English.

Accessibility basics

  • Readable contrast.
  • Mobile readability.
  • Keyboard-friendly interactions.
  • Alt text for meaningful images.
  • Captions or transcripts for audio/video.
  • No meaning carried by colour alone.

Offline action

  • Draw this.
  • Walk here.
  • Notice this.
  • Talk about this.
  • Print this.
  • Bring this to class or the library.

05 Council-Safe Version

A practical support position for councils, schools, libraries, and parents.

Why this matters for Council

Council can support creative digital layers when they are transparent, accessible, privacy-conscious, low-risk, and clearly linked to public value.

This charter helps avoid projects that look innovative but create hidden risks around child safety, privacy, accessibility, moderation, public trust, or attention extraction.

Why this matters for schools, libraries, and parents

Schools need teacher-led resources that do not create student accounts or privacy problems. Libraries need public activities that are easy to print, host, and explain. Parents need confidence that a tool will not collect child data or train compulsive use.

What Council can safely support

  • No-login QR learning pages for approved public art sites.
  • Printable lesson and poster packs.
  • Library workshop cards.
  • School-friendly classroom prompts.
  • Family walking prompts.
  • Anonymous pilot reporting.
  • Adult-mediated participation pathways.

What should be avoided

  • Tools that require children to create accounts.
  • Features that ask children to upload personal details.
  • Open public comments involving children.
  • Public leaderboards for children.
  • Gambling-style rewards or compulsive loops.
  • Digital experiences without a clear offline purpose.
  • Cultural content without permission and guidance.

07 Visual Layout Suggestions

Formal enough for Council. Clear enough for a classroom wall.

Formal charter PDF

6-8 pages: cover, definition, non-negotiables, who it protects, design rules, pilot checklist, success/failure, next actions.

1-page council/school summary

Use the line "Technology should give attention back" with a short checklist and the parent test.

Public poster

Headline: "Create, then leave the screen." Body copy should explain that healthy technology points attention back to Frankston.

Web treatment

Show the rules before decorative material. The rules are the value. Keep Blue Snake Studio to a maker stamp.

Use lightly

Bay horizon line, walking trail line, QR portal outline, worksheet tick marks, poster wall rhythm, cockatoo yellow accent.

Avoid

Mystical branding, heavy gradients, tech startup gloss, invented cultural symbols, or anything that makes the tool feel bigger than Frankston.
