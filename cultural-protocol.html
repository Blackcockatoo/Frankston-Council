Cultural Protocol

The first step is listening.

Frankston 2035 — Art That Lives is proposed on Bunurong and Boonwurrung Country. This page sets out the protocol that governs how this project handles all First Nations language, story, symbol, and cultural material.

No First Nations content has been created, claimed, or used in this proposal without permission. This page records what the correct pathway must be before any such content enters the project.

Permission first. No First Nations language, story, symbol, or cultural reference enters this project without guidance from the appropriate Traditional Owner organisations.
No extraction. Culture is not a resource to mine for creative effect. It belongs to the people it comes from.
No AI cultural claims. Artificial intelligence tools will not be used to generate, approximate, or simulate any First Nations language, image, or story.
Right to pause or stop. Any cultural partner has the unconditional right to pause, edit, or end their involvement at any stage.

Country

Frankston is on Bunurong and Boonwurrung Country.

The Bunurong and Boonwurrung peoples are the Traditional Custodians of the land on which Frankston stands, including the foreshore, the bay, and the land beyond. Their connection to Country is continuous and ongoing — not historical.

This proposal acknowledges that Country. It does not claim any special relationship with, connection to, or approval from Bunurong or Boonwurrung peoples or organisations. Acknowledgement is the floor, not the ceiling.

Any project activity on Country — including public art installation, signage, educational materials that reference place — must engage with the appropriate organisations before proceeding. That engagement is a relationship, not a checkbox.

What requires permission

The boundary is clear.

Language

Any word, phrase, song lyric, place name, or greeting in a First Nations language requires permission from the relevant language community and qualified speakers. This includes bilingual materials, learning packs, and any public-facing text.

Stories and knowledge

Creation stories, Country knowledge, seasonal knowledge, ceremonial knowledge, and any culturally specific narrative must come from and be authorised by the knowledge holders. They may not be summarised, paraphrased, or adapted without explicit consent.

Symbols and designs

Artistic symbols, totems, clan marks, and cultural imagery are not generic. Using them without guidance is misappropriation, regardless of intent. A visual that "looks like" Indigenous art is not a substitute for real collaboration.

Place names and geography

Traditional place names and Country descriptions carry meaning beyond translation. Using them in marketing, maps, educational materials, or public art requires guidance from the custodians of those names.

What not to do

Harm does not require bad intent.

Do not use AI to generate cultural content. No generative AI tool will be used to create, approximate, or simulate First Nations language, stories, art styles, or cultural references. AI cannot give permission. AI cannot understand cultural context. It can only produce plausible-sounding extraction.

Do not borrow aesthetic. Using dot patterns, ochre tones, or visual styles associated with First Nations art without permission and relationship is appropriation. The aesthetic belongs to a people.

Do not announce partnerships that do not exist. Nothing in this proposal implies an existing partnership, endorsement, or relationship with any First Nations organisation or individual. Any future partnership will be clearly attributed and genuinely collaborative.

Do not move fast. Respectful engagement takes time. A cold email or a rushed briefing is not the beginning of a relationship. The first step is an appropriate introduction, likely through existing networks.

Do not assume welcome. This project has not been invited. Before seeking involvement, it must understand what — if anything — would be useful or welcome from the perspective of the community, not from the perspective of the proposal.

The correct pathway

Permission-based, relationship-driven, and genuinely led.

The pathway for any future cultural engagement in this project is:

  1. Research the correct contact pathway — typically through the Bunurong Land Council Aboriginal Corporation or the relevant Registered Aboriginal Party for the area.
  2. Prepare an honest invitation brief — not a finished program to be endorsed, but a genuine request: is this something worth exploring together? What would make it worth exploring?
  3. Listen before proposing. The first meeting is for listening, not pitching. The community's interests lead. The project follows, if welcome.
  4. Agree on scope, rights, and attribution before anything is created. Who owns the output? Who has veto rights? Who is paid?
  5. Build review and exit into every stage. Cultural partners must be able to pause, redirect, or end the collaboration at any point without consequence.

This pathway is slow by design. It is not a barrier to the project — it is the project. The relationship is the outcome. Any cultural content is secondary to getting the relationship right.

Standing protocol

This applies to every version of this project, always.

  1. No First Nations language, story, symbol, or cultural content without explicit permission from the appropriate custodians.
  2. No AI generation of any cultural material, ever.
  3. No appropriation of visual style as a shortcut to cultural connection.
  4. No announcement of partnerships that have not been confirmed by the cultural partner.
  5. No use of cultural content without agreed attribution, rights, and payment where appropriate.
  6. Every cultural partner retains unconditional right to pause, edit, or withdraw participation.
  7. This protocol is not a ceiling. It is the minimum standard. If in doubt, stop and ask.
