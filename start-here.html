What it is

Public art that keeps working after the paint dries.

Frankston already has murals, a foreshore, library spaces, schools, and a sister-city relationship with Susono, Japan. Frankston 2035 — Art That Lives asks a simple question: what if all of that connected into a living creative system?

A mural becomes a lesson. A trail becomes a noticing exercise. A poster becomes a prompt for a child, teacher, artist, or resident to add something back. The sister-city relationship produces bilingual learning packs that students can actually use.

This is not a monument plan. Not a festival. Not an app. It is a low-cost, practical framework for building makers. The first move should be cheap, visible, testable, and easy to stop or improve.

The greatest public artwork is not a mural. It is a community that learns to create.

Who it's for

Anyone who can say yes to one small test.

Schools

Teachers and coordinators

Printable learning prompts linked to existing Frankston public art. No budget needed for the classroom step. One worksheet, one walk, one class session.

Libraries

Librarians and community workers

Creative display ideas, poster seeds, and community prompt concepts for library windows, walls, and program spaces.

Artists

Muralists and makers

A way to give existing work a second life as a teaching, noticing, or community-response prompt — without touching the mural itself.

Council

Officers and representatives

A framework ready to review. Nominate one pilot site. No budget commitment needed in Stage One. The first ask is a conversation.

Why it matters

Frankston is already the raw material.

The foreshore, the murals, the library, the schools — they're already here. This proposal does not ask Frankston to import a program. It asks Frankston to use what it has more fully.

A city that builds makers builds resilience. Art that teaches creates long-term participants, not passive audiences. Technology that gives attention back to real places treats residents like people, not metrics.

The ecosystem is the artwork. The first pilot is one small proof that it can work.

First pilot

The Living Mural Lesson Wall.

One mural, one learning layer, one school term. The simplest version: a printable A4 sheet beside an existing Frankston mural that invites students or visitors to look closely, respond creatively, and add something back.

A QR code card links to a short prompt page — no login, no tracking, no data collection. The sheet can be used in a classroom before the visit, at the mural during a walk, or after the visit back in school.

This is testable in one term. If it works, the next pilot scales. If it doesn't feel right, stop and learn from it.

Permissions and protocols

What needs checking before any pilot starts.

First Nations content: Nothing on this site uses Bunurong / Boonwurrung language, story, or symbol without permission. Any future cultural content must go through the appropriate Traditional Owner organisations. This is not optional — it is the basis for any work on Country.

Sister-city content: Any Japanese-language or Susono-related material needs review by the existing sister-city custodians and qualified speakers before public use.

Student work: Any student artwork or photography needs parent/guardian and school consent before appearing publicly. Schools determine their own consent pathways.

Public art sites: Adding physical components near or on existing public art needs the relevant site-owner and council permission. Poster seeds and classroom packs need no permission. A physical install does.

What to do next

Choose one small pilot and test it.

  • 1
    Read the pilot options. Five low-cost pilot ideas. Pick one that fits where you are right now. No funding commitment required for Stage One.
  • 2
    Print one poster seed. Download an A4 poster from the Poster Seeds page. Print it. Put it somewhere in Frankston. See if anyone stops to read it.
  • 3
    Run one classroom or community test. Use a noticing walk, a creative prompt, or a library display. No budget required for the classroom step.
  • 4
    Note what you'd do differently. The first test is for learning, not for publishing. Honest feedback shapes Stage Two.
  • 5
    Share what you found. If you want to take it further — if you're a council officer, teacher, artist, or community group — get in touch. The first ask is one conversation.
