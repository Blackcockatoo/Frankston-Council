Schools

Make Frankston easier to learn from.

Frankston 2035 gives teachers a practical way to connect public art, local place, student creativity, and safe technology — without a specialist budget or new equipment.

Students can learn from a mural, a beach walk, a family memory, a station route, a library display, or a school artwork wall. The digital layer should only help them find the prompt and then return them to making.

Student artwork pathway

Celebrate student work without rushing it online.

  1. Students create work from a local prompt — no personal data, no login.
  2. Teacher reviews for school policy, respect, privacy, and accessibility.
  3. Family consent is gathered before any public display.
  4. Display starts classroom-only or library-hosted, then scales up only after approval.
  5. Any public QR layer must be adult-led, no-login by default, and reviewed against the Healthy Technology Charter.

Safe technology framing

Create, then leave the screen.

A school-safe technology layer should be short, optional, accessible, printable, and adult-led. It should never require student accounts by default or collect personal data from children.

Hard rules:

  • No infinite scroll.
  • No gambling mechanics or streak pressure.
  • No public student comments by default.
  • No personal data collection from children.
  • Clear ending and offline creative action on every page.

Before any public pilot

Use this checklist before any child-facing activity goes live.

  1. Confirm the one real-world action the activity supports.
  2. Remove any feature that keeps children scrolling or playing after the task is done.
  3. Use no-login access by default.
  4. Avoid child personal data entirely.
  5. Add a clear ending and offline next step.
  6. Check accessibility basics: contrast, mobile, keyboard, alt text.
  7. Provide a printable version that works without the digital layer.
  8. Gather school and family consent before any public display of student work.
