Pilots — Pilot 01 full brief

Living Mural Lesson Wall

One existing artwork. One lesson layer. One school term. Under $500.

This is the most practical first pilot in the Frankston 2035 proposal. It does not build a new mural. It takes a wall that already exists and adds a simple, low-tech learning layer — a weatherproof sign, a printable worksheet, and a clear classroom connection.

A teacher brings a class to a foreshore mural. The sign gives them one question. The worksheet gives them one task. The wall becomes a lesson. That is enough to begin.

Who it's for

The people who make this work.

Councillors and council officers

A low-risk, low-cost entry point. No new infrastructure. Works with a wall you already own or manage. Evaluate after one term before committing to anything more.

Teachers and school coordinators

A single off-site excursion activity with a pre-made worksheet. Curriculum-connectable. No login. No app. No student data collected.

Librarians and community workers

A print-first resource table or walk-and-notice activity. The wall is the venue. The worksheet is the program. Can run without visiting the physical wall if needed.

Artists and artwork holders

An invitation to have your existing work teach. You are consulted before any lesson layer is added. You can withdraw at any time with no obligation.

Permissions first

Nothing goes up without this checklist.

  1. Artist permission. Contact the original artist. Explain the lesson concept. Get written agreement before any sign, worksheet, or web page references the work.
  2. Site and signage approval. Check who owns or manages the wall. Temporary signage in most council-managed spaces requires a simple approval. Allow 2–4 weeks.
  3. School or group consent. Teachers handle excursion consent through school's standard process. No extra form is needed unless student work will be displayed publicly.
  4. Cultural protocol check. If the artwork includes any First Nations reference, or the site has cultural significance, stop here. Do not proceed until guidance is obtained from an appropriate Traditional Owner organisation.
  5. Privacy review. No student names, face photos, or personal details on the sign, the worksheet, or the web page. First name or anonymous credit only if student work is displayed.
  6. QR test. If a QR code is used, test it on at least two phones before printing the sign. Label it clearly as a short link to a lesson prompt — not a commercial link.

Teacher / librarian note

What you need before the visit.

Time required: 20–40 minutes on site. Extend with a classroom follow-up if you want.

What to bring: Printed worksheets (one per student or pair), pencils, the teacher feedback form.

On the day: Gather the group in front of the wall. Read the sign question aloud. Give students 60 seconds to just look before they pick up a pencil. The worksheet has three prompts — look, sketch, respond. There is no wrong answer.

After the visit: Students can share one response with the class or take the worksheet home. Return the anonymous feedback form within one week of the visit. That feedback shapes the next version.

Curriculum connections: Visual arts (observation, response to artworks); English (descriptive and reflective writing); local studies (Frankston community and place); environment (foreshore and public space).

What not to do: Do not photograph student faces without consent. Do not ask students for their surname, home address, or contact details. Do not use the lesson to promote any commercial service.

Student worksheet outline

Three prompts. One page. Pencils only.

1. Look

Stand in front of the wall. Look at it for 60 seconds without writing. Find one detail you would not have noticed from a photo. Write or draw what you found.

2. Sketch

Draw one small part of the artwork. It does not have to be perfect. Choose something that interests you — a shape, a colour, a line, a creature, a face, an edge.

3. Respond

Finish one sentence: "This wall makes me think about..." or "If I could add one thing here it would be..." or "The most interesting part is..."

Teacher note (on reverse)

What went well? What confused students? What would make this more useful next time? Three questions, half a page. Return within one week. Anonymous is fine.

The worksheet is intentionally minimal. The wall does the teaching. The worksheet is the prompt. Keep it short enough that students finish before the group is ready to leave.

Healthy tech check

Use this before any QR or web layer goes live.

  1. The lesson works fully as a printed worksheet. The QR code or URL is optional.
  2. The web page (if used) has no login, no child data collection, and no infinite scroll.
  3. The QR code is tested on two phones before it is printed on the sign.
  4. The sign clearly states what the QR links to.
  5. The web page has a clear ending: "You're done. Close this and draw, walk, or talk."
  6. No analytics that identify individual visitors.
  7. No commercial link, ad, or sign-up prompt anywhere on the page.

Offline activity

What happens away from the wall.

The pilot is designed to be offline-first. The wall is the experience. The worksheet is the prompt. The classroom follow-up is where the reflection happens.

Back in class or the library: Students share one response each. The group discusses: what did different people notice? Did anyone notice the same detail? What question would you want answered about this wall?

Optional extension: Students write or draw a response they'd want to see added to the wall. Collected responses can be displayed as a classroom poster or shared with the artist — with permission.

For library groups: Use the worksheet as a drop-in activity at a table, with a printed or projected image of the artwork. A physical visit is better; this is the fallback when that isn't possible.

Feedback questions

Three questions for the teacher or group leader.

1. Did students engage?

Did most students engage with the worksheet? Was there a question or prompt they found confusing or too easy?

2. What would you change?

What would make this more useful for your group? A different wall? A shorter worksheet? A different prompt? More time on site?

3. Would you return?

Would you bring a group to this wall again? If yes, what would make a second visit worthwhile?

Return the form

Anonymous is fine. Return within one week of the visit by email or post. Results shape the next version of the lesson layer.

Next step

One real action from here.

Nominate one candidate wall. Work through the permission checklist. Contact the artist or site owner with a one-paragraph explanation: one sign, one worksheet, one school term, one group visit, no cost to them, no obligation to continue.

That conversation is the first real step. Nothing is printed until the artist says yes.
