Vision

Useful public art for a more creative Frankston.

Frankston 2035 is a practical civic creativity framework. It connects art, place, schools, libraries, healthy technology, and community participation so more people can become makers — not just viewers.

It is not a single mural, app, festival, or brand campaign. It is a way to turn public creativity into repeatable local infrastructure.

What it is

A framework that gives existing assets a second life.

Frankston has spent years investing in public art, foreshore activation, and creative placemaking. Most of that investment has a natural ceiling: a mural is seen once by most people who pass it, then becomes wallpaper. The civic and educational value is largely locked inside the paint.

Frankston 2035 adds one new capability to what already exists: the ability to teach, travel, and be added to. A mural becomes a lesson. A trail becomes a noticing exercise. A library pack becomes a prompt for a class, a family, or a community worker to create something real and contribute it back.

The four loops

The ecosystem is the artwork.

1

Wall to lesson

Public art becomes a worksheet, classroom prompt, library activity, or local maths and pattern exercise — used by real teachers in real classrooms.

2

Walk to attention

QR codes and trail cards point people back to the place: look up, notice, sketch, listen, then keep walking. The screen is a doorway, not the destination.

3

Exchange to artifact

Sister-city material becomes a replyable object: a song, poster, class pack, or bilingual learning prompt — reviewed and approved, not decorative symbolism.

4

Viewer to maker

Residents and students add work back into the system through guided, moderated, low-risk contribution pathways. The audience becomes the creator.

Why now

Frankston already has everything it needs.

Frankston schools are looking for digital learning tools that are safe, local, and genuinely engaging — not another generic app. The Frankston-Susono sister-city relationship is real but under-resourced with shared content. There is growing civic appetite to involve First Nations communities in public space on respectful, consent-based terms.

These threads — public art, education, sister-city connection, and First Nations respect — are usually treated as four separate council line items. This proposal treats them as one opportunity: give Frankston's public art a second life, and all four threads gain a place to meet.

The ask

Three things. None require a budget decision today.

  1. Note this proposal and indicate whether there is appetite to proceed to a Stage One pilot.
  2. Nominate a single candidate location — an existing mural, foreshore site, or trail — for the pilot, in consultation with relevant council officers.
  3. Request the full pilot project sheets for a detailed, scoped Stage One scope.

That is the entire ask at this stage. Nothing else is being requested of Council today.

Content principles

The ten rules that protect the vision.

  1. Frankston is the hero.
  2. Blue Snake Studio is the engine, maker, or stamp — not the headline.
  3. Start small, think generational.
  4. Every big idea needs a low-cost pilot.
  5. Art must be useful, not just beautiful.
  6. Technology must give attention back to real places and real people.
  7. First Nations content is permission-based, respectful, and guided by appropriate Traditional Owner organisations.
  8. Sister City content must focus on real exchange, not symbolic decoration.
  9. Every page points to practical next steps.
  10. The ecosystem is the artwork.
