# 07 - Healthy Technology
## Council-Safe Version

## Draft policy position

Frankston 2035 - Art That Lives may include digital resources that support public art, education, libraries, family participation, local walking trails, poster activity, and community creative learning.

Any digital resource associated with the project should be designed to be privacy-conscious, accessible, low-risk, and supportive of real-world participation.

The guiding principle is:

Technology should give attention back.

## Practical meaning

Digital tools should help people engage with Frankston, not replace engagement with Frankston.

Project technology should:

- support public learning and participation
- work without login wherever possible
- avoid collecting personal data from children
- include offline creative prompts
- be accessible and readable on common devices
- provide printable alternatives where useful
- include clear endings and next steps
- use anonymous aggregate metrics only where needed for evaluation

## Child and school safety position

Child-facing experiences should not ask children to create accounts, provide contact details, upload personal information, or participate in open public commenting systems.

Where school or youth participation is involved, the preferred model is:

- adult-led
- classroom-safe
- no-login
- printable
- opt-in
- low data
- clear about permissions
- easy to explain to parents and carers

## What should be avoided

The project should not use:

- infinite scroll
- dark patterns
- gambling-style mechanics
- reward loops designed to increase compulsive use
- default logins for public participation
- child data collection
- public child leaderboards
- unclear consent flows
- unmoderated child submissions
- digital experiences without a clear offline purpose

## Public art position

Digital layers connected to public art should add practical value.

Appropriate uses may include:

- short artist or site notes where permissions allow
- learning prompts
- walking prompts
- printable activities
- classroom extensions
- accessibility support
- event or workshop information

Digital layers should not make the artwork a gimmick, obscure the artist, or convert public space into a promotional funnel.

## Libraries and families

Library and family resources should be simple, accessible, printable, and easy to run without specialist technical support.

Resources should help people:

- notice local places
- make something
- discuss an idea
- use a worksheet
- join a workshop
- take a creative action offline

## Cultural protocol

Any First Nations content must be permission-based, respectful, and guided by appropriate Traditional Owner organisations.

The project should not invent, simulate, decorate with, or approximate First Nations language, stories, symbols, or cultural material.

Sister City content should focus on real exchange, learning, translation where appropriate, and relationship, not symbolic decoration.

## Council can safely support

Subject to normal review, Council could safely support:

- a no-login QR learning page for one approved public art or civic site
- a printable classroom or library prompt pack
- an adult-led workshop resource
- a family walking prompt
- a short pilot with anonymous aggregate usage reporting
- a review process using the Healthy Technology Charter before launch

## Blue Snake Studio must demonstrate

B$S should demonstrate that it can:

- keep Frankston as the central public value
- build simple and fast digital resources
- avoid unnecessary data collection
- prevent child-facing risk by design
- provide offline alternatives
- support accessibility basics
- write plain public-facing instructions
- report pilot results without identifying children or families

## Success indicators

Possible indicators include:

- number of schools, libraries, or community partners willing to trial the resource
- number of printable resources distributed
- anonymous page visits or downloads where needed
- qualitative feedback from teachers, librarians, families, and Council officers
- evidence of offline creative activity
- clear absence of privacy, moderation, or duty-of-care incidents

## Stop or redesign indicators

The pilot should be stopped or redesigned if:

- children are asked for personal data
- participation requires accounts without a strong reviewed reason
- the experience encourages prolonged or compulsive screen use
- schools or libraries cannot safely explain or manage it
- privacy or consent language is unclear
- accessibility basics are missing
- cultural or site permissions are unresolved
- B$S branding overtakes the civic purpose
