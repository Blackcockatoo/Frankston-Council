# 07 - Healthy Technology
## 1-Page Summary

## Core thesis

Technology should give attention back.

Frankston 2035 - Art That Lives uses technology only where it helps people notice, make, learn, walk, discuss, print, share, or participate in real places. The screen is a doorway, not the destination.

## Definition

Healthy technology is short, clear, useful, privacy-first, accessible, and connected to offline creativity.

It helps children, families, schools, libraries, artists, and Council do something practical without turning Frankston into a feed.

## Non-negotiables

- No infinite scroll
- No dark patterns
- No gambling mechanics
- No login by default
- No personal data collection from children
- Short-session design
- Clear endings
- Offline creative prompts
- Family and classroom modes
- Accessibility basics
- Anonymous aggregate metrics only where needed
- "Create, then leave the screen" principle

## What the user journey should feel like

1. Scan or open.
2. Understand the prompt quickly.
3. Make, notice, walk, draw, discuss, or print.
4. Finish.

## Why this matters for councils

Council can support creative digital tools when they are low-risk, explainable, privacy-conscious, accessible, and linked to clear public value.

This charter helps Council avoid supporting tools that look innovative but create hidden risks around child safety, privacy, moderation, accessibility, public trust, or attention extraction.

## Why this matters for schools and libraries

Schools and libraries need resources that work in group settings, do not require child accounts, do not collect child data, and can be used without complex technical support.

Healthy technology should help a teacher run a lesson, help a librarian run a session, and help a family do something creative together.

## What Council can safely support

- No-login QR learning layers
- Printable poster and worksheet packs
- Family walking prompts
- Teacher-led classroom activities
- Library workshop prompts
- Anonymous aggregate reporting
- Adult-mediated submissions where needed

## What Blue Snake Studio must prove

B$S must prove it can deliver useful public technology without attention traps, child-data collection, compulsive mechanics, or brand-first design.

The studio is the engine. Frankston is the hero.

## Success looks like

- A child makes something offline.
- A teacher can use it safely.
- A library can print and host it.
- A family can understand it quickly.
- A public artwork becomes more useful.
- Council can explain the risk controls.
- Frankston becomes more creative, not more screen-bound.

## Failure looks like

- The tool increases passive screen time.
- It asks children for personal data.
- It needs accounts before offering value.
- It uses gambling-style or compulsive patterns.
- It creates unclear privacy or moderation risk.
- It makes B$S the centre instead of Frankston.

## Ruthless test

If a feature steals attention, cut it.

If it needs child data, redesign it.

If it encourages compulsive use, reject it.

If it cannot be explained to a parent in one sentence, simplify it.
